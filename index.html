What Is MGUSD

MGUSD is MoneyGram's native U.S. dollar stablecoin, launched June 2, 2026 and issued and deployed on the Stellar blockchain at launch. It is integrated directly into the MoneyGram app as a self-custodial wallet, giving customers a stable, dollar-denominated balance they can hold and access 24/7, move globally, and convert into local currency when they choose.

"MGUSD is not a stablecoin project. It is MoneyGram's strategy to give millions of customers access to a stable digital dollar balance connected to one of the world's largest global money movement and cash access networks."
— Luke Tuttle, MoneyGram Chief Product & Technology Officer, "Why We Launched MGUSD" (June 11, 2026)
Core idea: A token only comes into existence when a real dollar stands behind it, and is destroyed ("burned") when that dollar is paid back out. Bridge (a Stripe company) is the regulated, GENIUS Act-ready issuer; M0's smart contracts mint and burn the tokens on Stellar; Crossmint provides the API layer for the consumer wallet; Fireblocks powers MoneyGram's stablecoin corporate treasury; and MoneyGram owns the customer relationship and the global cash network.

Why a Balance, Not Just a Transaction

For most of its history, MoneyGram was associated with a single transaction — send money, receive money, pay a bill. Those services remain core. What changes with MGUSD is that a customer can now hold a stable, dollar-denominated balance in the app. A balance can support remittance, but it can also support spending, receiving, converting, accessing cash, and a broader set of financial services many people have never been able to easily use. MoneyGram frames this as a step beyond prepaid debit cards — which solved access for people without a bank account but often came with high fees and a narrow set of services — toward lower fees and a wider range of services.

Who it's for: MoneyGram has long served underbanked and unbanked consumers — people who might pick up cash and, within a single day, lose meaningful value to local-currency volatility. A stable dollar balance lets them choose when and how to receive funds, hold value, track transactions, and spend on critical needs. In Tuttle's words: "We're not just giving the consumer access; we're giving the consumer control."

How MGUSD Works: Minted In, Burned Out

Rather than fix a strict step order between partners, the clearest way to understand MGUSD is the two operations behind every balance. The exact sequence in which partners hand off isn't published — and it matters less than the rule it enforces: every MGUSD is created against a real dollar and destroyed when that dollar is paid out, so circulating supply always matches reserves.

↓ Money In — "Mint"

A customer funds their MoneyGram balance. 100 MGUSD is minted (created) on Stellar and delivered into the app as a spendable, self-custodial balance — available 24/7. Creation happens only because a real dollar is held in reserve behind it.

Who's involved: MoneyGram (funding & app) · M0 (mint contracts) · Bridge (reserve backing) · Crossmint (delivers balance to wallet) · Stellar (records it)

↑ Money Out — "Burn"

A customer redeems. That 100 MGUSD is burned (permanently destroyed) on Stellar and the reserve dollar is released — paid out as cash or local currency through MoneyGram's global network, on the customer's terms.

Who's involved: MoneyGram (redeem & payout) · Crossmint (releases from wallet) · M0 (burn contracts) · Bridge (releases reserve) · Stellar (records it)

The invariant: Mint and burn keep MGUSD fully reserve-backed at all times — supply rises only when dollars come in and falls when dollars go out. Anyone can verify mint and burn activity on Stellar in real time. (The "who's involved" lists name each contributor, not a fixed order of operations.)

The Six Partners & Technologies — What Each Provides

The heart of MGUSD is what each partner and technology contributes. They work together as layers — not a single rigid line — each owning a distinct part of the system, so trust never rests on any one company's word. Roles below match MoneyGram's own descriptions in "Why We Launched MGUSD."

MoneyGram Distribution & Treasury
VALUE: the trusted brand, customers, and the cash on/off-ramp

The customer experience end to end — the app, identity verification, funding, and the final payout into your hands or your recipient's. MoneyGram serves over 60 million active customers through nearly 500,000 retail locations worldwide (more than 70% of transactions now digital), and operates the largest crypto-to-cash on/off-ramp network in the world. Backed by more than eight decades of cross-border money movement, with compliance — KYC, sanctions screening, transaction monitoring, consumer protection — built into the product, not bolted on.

M0 Smart Contracts
VALUE: the auditable engine that creates & destroys tokens

The on-chain math. MGUSD tokens are minted and burned using M0's smart-contract infrastructure, deployed on Stellar at launch. When issuance is authorized, the contracts create exactly that amount — and burns destroy exactly what's redeemed, all publicly auditable. M0 is a modular stablecoin platform with more than $300 million in on-chain supply across other issuers.

Bridge (a Stripe company) Regulated Issuer
VALUE: the regulated dollar backing behind every token

Issuance and regulatory integrity. Bridge — the stablecoin platform Stripe acquired for $1.1 billion in February 2025 — is MGUSD's "regulated, GENIUS Act-ready issuer." The GENIUS Act (signed July 18, 2025) is the first U.S. federal stablecoin law: it requires 100% reserve backing with dollars or other low-risk liquid assets, monthly public reserve disclosures, and restricts payment-stablecoin issuance to licensed issuers once its rules take effect. Keeping every MGUSD good for a dollar is Bridge's job.

Crossmint Consumer Wallet API
VALUE: turns an on-chain token into a usable in-app balance

The consumer-wallet layer. Crossmint is the API layer for managing the MoneyGram consumer wallet and orchestrating transfers of MGUSD within the MoneyGram platform. It delivers MGUSD into the customer's self-custodial wallet and releases it on redemption — so customers experience a simple balance, not blockchain mechanics.

Fireblocks Corporate Treasury
VALUE: secure custody & controls for MoneyGram's own funds

The institutional treasury backbone. In MoneyGram's words, Fireblocks "powers MoneyGram's stablecoin corporate treasury" — the secure custody and controls behind MoneyGram's own money operations, not the consumer wallet. This is the enterprise layer that lets treasury, finance, and operations manage value held on-chain alongside value held in bank accounts — the same treasury function that supports prefunding, settlement, and FX.

Stellar Blockchain
VALUE: the fast, cheap, public ledger of record

The immutable public record. MGUSD is issued and deployed on Stellar at launch — every mint, transfer, and burn is written permanently to a blockchain purpose-built for payments, with ~5-second ledger closes and fees of fractions of a cent. MoneyGram and the Stellar Development Foundation have partnered since 2021 on stablecoin-powered money movement; MGUSD is the next chapter of that five-year partnership.

The Treasury & Settlement Layer (Behind the Scenes)

MGUSD is also part of a broader modernization of MoneyGram's infrastructure to use stablecoins where they bring benefit. This layer is separate from the consumer wallet — it's how MoneyGram runs its own money operations, and it's where Fireblocks sits.

Stablecoin treasury, embedded into enterprise finance

MoneyGram has augmented its stablecoin treasury function and integrated it into its enterprise financial platforms, so cash and stablecoin holdings are managed together with standard workflows, approvals, controls, and reporting. Notably, MoneyGram is stablecoin-agnostic for prefunding and settlement — it uses stablecoins other than MGUSD when trading with partners and exchanges — preserving choice for agents and partners. As of the June 11 article, MoneyGram reports it is on pace to do FX trading using stablecoins at roughly a $2 billion annual run rate.

Why a Self-Custodial Wallet

MoneyGram deliberately chose a self-custodial wallet architecture — the digital asset sits in a wallet controlled by the customer while MoneyGram provides the trusted experience. Per the article, this is a KYC-gated wallet, not an open DeFi wallet: it is built for the customer who needs to send $200 to family, receive value, hold it safely, convert it when needed, and access it through a network they already know and trust — not for moving assets into arbitrary external protocols. By issuing MGUSD itself, MoneyGram says it can align incentives, economics, and value around its customers and partners rather than being constrained by another issuer's coin economics.

What the Customer Sees vs. What's Underneath

The experience: a familiar balance — the technology "disappears into the experience"

A customer just sees a dollar balance and activity in the MoneyGram app. Underneath, the balance is a real entry on the public Stellar ledger: Crossmint's API turns the on-chain token into the in-app balance, the wallet is self-custodial (the customer's keys control the funds), and anyone can verify supply and flows on a public Stellar explorer — addresses are pseudonymous, so identity stays private with MoneyGram's KYC records. As Tuttle puts it, customers shouldn't have to think about the underlying technology at all.

Key Takeaway

MGUSD is not a coin for its own sake — it's MoneyGram using stablecoin technology to give customers a stable digital dollar balance on a network they already trust. The value lives in what each partner contributes: MoneyGram brings the brand, customers, and cash network; Bridge the regulated dollar backing; M0 the auditable mint/burn engine; Crossmint the usable in-app wallet; Fireblocks the corporate treasury; and Stellar the fast, public ledger. Each owns a distinct layer, so the system's trust doesn't rest on any single company's word.

Why it matters: MGUSD doesn't try to win by being everywhere first. It's built to be useful inside a network that already has real customer demand — pairing radical on-chain transparency (supply, mints, and burns are public on Stellar in real time) with pseudonymous identity kept private at MoneyGram, and the discipline of a regulated, compliance-first global financial company.
